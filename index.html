About the Workshop

19th International Conference on Parallel Problem Solving From Nature
August 29 - September 2, 2026, Trento, Italy

Quantum Computing (QC) is a computation paradigm that leverages the peculiar properties of quantum mechanics such as superposition and entanglement. Several QC paradigms exist but the two principal ones would be the gate-based and the adiabatic one. The first one describes the computation as quantum circuits that acts on the qubits using some set of quantum gates, while the second paradigm is based on the adiabatic theorem in order to solve discrete optimisation problems. Each of the paradigms has some advantages and shortfalls and a range of applicability, where a plethora of perspectives rises.
QC has a wide domain of applications, but one of the principal and most promising is optimisation-problem solving, where QC algorithms are proven to provide, in some cases, very promising theoretical and practical speedup regarding the classical computation techniques. On the other hand, classical optimisation (e.g., Evolutionary Computation (EC), etc.) is also proven to be a promising alternative for certain classes of tasks as well. Thus, it is widely believed that QC will not replace classical computation but instead will collaboratively work together, taking advantage of one another's strengths. Indeed, efforts in the community are made to enhance classical methods using quantum routines, or on the reverse, enhance quantum processes using classical optimisation and ultimately, design fully quantum, hybrid quantum-classical or quantum-inspired optimisers for efficiently solving optimisation problems. In this same line of thought, this workshop focuses on works that explore how QC can take advantage of classical optimisation, and vice versa, demonstrating synergy domains and also the limits of the capabilities of each of them. Specifically, the scope of this workshop covers the development of quantum, quantum-inspired, and hybrid quantum classical optimisation algorithms, their application to real-world problems, and the enhancement of quantum routines (e.g. transpiling) with classical optimisation techniques. In this same line of thoughts, this workshop topics include but are not limited to:

  • The design of quantum, hybrid and quantum-inspired optimisation techniques.
  • Optimisation-problem solving using quantum, hybrid and quantum-inspired algorithms.
  • Classical and Quantum optimisers for quantum simulation.
  • Classical and Qauntum optimisers for embedding in adiabatic quantum computers.
  • Classical and Qauntum for transpilation in gate-based quantum computers.
  • Quantum variational algorithms.
  • etc.

Submission and Participation Guidelines

  • ABOUT: extended abstracts, positional statements/papers, reviews/summaries/comments/techniques,
  • PAGE LIMIT: up to 4 pages (references included).
  • TEMPLATE: It is recommended to use the same template at the one used in the main conference PPSN.
  • SUBMISSION PLATFORM: Submissions will be done through the following LINK .
  • REVIEWS: Each paper will be reviewed in a double-blind review process. The submitted papers should be anonymised. All information that can allow identifying the authors should be removed. This includes author names, affiliations, and acknowledgments.
  • CAMERA READY: The authors of the accepted papers are expected to submit a camera ready version by the camerea-ready deadline.
  • REGISTRATION: At least one of the authors are expected to register, by the registration deadline, to participate in the conference.
  • PRESENTATION: The authors of the accepted papers are expected to deliver a 15 minutes presentation followed by 5 minutes of Q&As.
  • PARTICIPATION: It is expected that at least one of the authors will be present the work in-person at the conference.
  • NOTE: Submissions will not be published in the conference proceedings.

About the Organisers

Zakaria A. DAHI: Dr. Zakaria A. Dahi is a tenured researcher at INRIA and a lecturer at the Department of Computer Science at the University of Lille. His research focuses on the design of quantum-classical algorithms for combinatorial optimisation. This includes finding the synergies and boundaries between the quantum and classical paradigms. He has published works in several well-established journals and conferences. He has an experience in scientific research and teaching at the university level. He has been actively working, participating and elaborating several national and international research projects on classical and quantum combinatorial optimisation. He has also participated in the organisation of several national and international conferences.

Pascal Halffmann: Dr. Pascal Halffmann holds a PhD in mathematics, specifically in mathematical optimization. He is currently a researcher at Fraunhofer ITWM, where he is in the leading role as research coordinator for the quantum computing activities of the institute. His research focuses on optimization covering the development and application of optimization algorithms and related techniques e.g., from machine learning. He is a proven expert in multiobjective and robust optimization. Application areas cover energy, finance, logistics, and supply chain. Since 2021, he has shifted his research towards quantum computing with particular emphasis on quantum and hybrid quantum–classical algorithms for combinatorial, multiobjective, and robust optimization problems. His work explores both the potential and the limitations of near-term quantum hardware, including variational quantum algorithms, quantum annealing, and QUBO-based approaches, also in application domains such as energy systems or finance. His expertise is underlined by a strong publication record including 16 peer-reviewed articles in impact journals, book chapters and one book. He has been part of several national and international conferences, where he contributed with 14 talks and additionally organized two sessions. Further, he was part of the organizing committee of one workshop and one conference. In addition to conferences, he has provided 17 invited talks at universities, industry events, and industry clients. He has been involved in seven research projects; in four projects and an additional industry project, he took over the role of project manager/ principal investigator. He has acquired four research projects with a total funding volume of more than four million euros. He has supervised or co-supervised seven bachelor’s and master’s theses in the areas of quantum computing, optimization, and applied mathematics, and currently co-supervised two PhD theses (multiobjective robust optimization and quantum optimization). He has further teaching expertise with six lectures, given at RPTU, several tutorials, and by providing lectures, talks, and workshops for industry clients at Fraunhofer ITWM.
